Part I: Country-level social costs of carbon (CSCC)

This part shows country-level social costs of carbon (CSCC), which measure the monetary damages caused by emitting one additional ton of CO₂, attributed to each country.

Methodology & data sources
  • Estimation following empirical data approach of Ricke et al. (2018).
  • Updated damage function from Harding et al. (2023): incorporating convergence growth effects from neoclassical growth theory.
  • Estimates based on country-level economic data (World Bank) and climate data (Willmott and Matsuura 2012).
  • Main scenario: SSP2–RCP6.0 with a 2.5% discount rate.
Notes on interpretation
  • Shades of red indicate higher damages; shades of blue indicate potential net benefits.
  • Countries with no data are shown in grey.
  • Values are in 2020 USD.

Part II: Fluxes of the terrestrial carbon cycle Fk

This part shows country-level carbon fluxes which describe how much carbon is removed from or added to the atmosphere.

Definitions, data sources & sign convention

Global Carbon Budget (2013–2022 decadal mean):

  • Natural land sink (from forests) (StFLAND): Net CO₂ uptake by forests, based on DGVMs from the TRENDY intercomparison project.
  • Land-use change emissions (ELUC): Net CO₂ emissions/sequestration from land use, land-use change, and forestry (LULUCF), derived from bookkeeping models.
  • Fossil fuel emissions (EFOS): Emissions from combustion, cement production, and minor industrial sources, derived from global energy and industrial statistics.

Aggregate measures:

  • NtFLAND = StFLAND + ELUC (net land flux)
  • NtFTOT = StFLAND + ELUC + EFOS (net total flux)

Sign convention: Negative = net uptake (sink); Positive = net emissions (source).

Part III: Comprehensive Wealth and Investment for country i

This part shows carbon-flux contributions to Comprehensive Investment (CCI) under different accounting perspectives (global, domestic, outbound, inbound, and the net balance).

Interpretation: positive = net contributor to the global carbon commons; negative = net beneficiary.

Concept: Comprehensive wealth & investment (Arrow et al.)

Following Arrow et al. (2003), intergenerational well-being is determined by an economy’s comprehensive wealth, which represents the aggregate value of all capital assets.

These assets include: manufactured capital, human capital (e.g., health, knowledge, skills), and natural and resource-based capital.

Vi(t) = r(t)t + Σ pi(t)Ki(t) + Σ gi,l(t)Gl(t)

The change in comprehensive wealth over time is called Comprehensive Investment.

Carbon accounting: how CCI is constructed

Carbon fluxes change the atmospheric carbon stock:

ΔGCatm = Fk

Units: GtC/yr

And the shadow price for country i is given by its CSCC:

gi = −CSCCi · 3.67

Units: billion US$/GtC

Comprehensive Investment in terms of carbon fluxes is then:

ΔVi(t) = gi · Fk,i

Units: billion US$/yr

  • Emissions increase atmospheric CO₂ (Fk > 0) ⇒ public bad
  • Sequestration reduces atmospheric CO₂ (Fk < 0) ⇒ public good
Measures shown (Global / Domestic / Outbound / Inbound / Balance)

For flux Fk,i:

  • Global CCI: Fk,i · Σj gj, domestic flux multiplied by global SCC
  • Domestic CCI: Fk,i · gi, domestic flux multiplied by domestic CSCC
  • Outbound CCI: Fk,i · Σj≠i gj, domestic flux multiplied by sum of other countries’ CSCCs
  • Inbound CCI: Σj≠i Fk,j · gi, other countries’ fluxes multiplied by domestic CSCC
  • Balance of Transboundary CCI: CCIk,iOutbound − CCIk,iInbound
